2. THE STIFFNESS PROBLEM
========================

2.1 Root cause: n_e jumps 8 orders per pulse cycle

  ON phase:  n_e peaks at ~1e16 (VI curve) or ~3e14 (parametric)
  OFF phase: n_e decays to ~1e8 (floor) via 3-body attachment
  Jump ratio: 1e8 → 1e16 = 8 orders (VI) or 1e8 → 3e14 = 6 orders (parametric)

This jump creates extreme stiffness at every ON→OFF and OFF→ON transition.

2.2 Why n_e decays so fast in afterglow

  Primary loss: 3-body attachment e + O2 + M → O2- + M
  Cross section (σ/N) peaks at 0.08 eV = near thermal Te
  At thermal Te (0.026 eV), k_eff = k_raw × N_gas ≈ 5e-11 cm³/s
  With [O2] = 3.6e18 cm⁻³: ν_att = k × [O2] ≈ 1.8e8 s⁻¹ → τ ≈ 5.5 ns

  So n_e drops by factor e every ~5.5 ns after Te reaches thermal.
  From 1e16 to 1e8 = 18.4 e-folds ≈ 100 ns of thermal decay.

2.3 Te collapse timescale

  Energy relaxation: τ_ε = ε̄ / (N_gas × A22) ≈ 17 ps at eps=3eV
  But A22 decreases with eps, so actual cooling: ~1-50 ns
  Literature confirms: Te collapses to thermal in 1-50 ns at atmospheric pressure
  (Bieniek 2021, Lepikhin 2017, Jiang 2019)


3. SOLVER APPROACHES TESTED
============================

3.1 Constrained BDF (manual stepping) — CURRENT DEFAULT

  Method: scipy BDF stepper with per-step clamping of n_e and ne_eps
  
  WITHOUT analytical Jacobian (FD):
    - Works but 67 RHS per Jacobian evaluation
    - 11,000 RHS/pulse → 266 hours for 340k pulses
    - Overflow warnings from FD Jacobian at large n_e gradients
    
  WITH analytical Jacobian:
    - BDF takes 100 steps with ZERO progress (t stays at 0)
    - Newton iteration never converges
    - Root cause: Jacobian condition number ≈ 10^75
    - I - h*J is near-singular for ANY step size h

  VERDICT: FD works but slow. Analytical Jac doesn't work due to conditioning.

3.2 solve_ivp(BDF) per pulse segment — OPERATOR SPLIT

  ON phase: solve_ivp(BDF, jac=analytical) for 18µs
  OFF phase: solve_ivp(BDF, FD) with frozen electrons for 82µs

  Performance: 838 RHS/pulse (ON: 908 + OFF: 13), 308ms/pulse
  ETA: 29 hours for 340k pulses
  
  Issues:
    - BDF restart overhead: each solve_ivp call starts from order 1
    - ON phase: 56 Jacobian evaluations per 18µs segment
    - Frozen electrons: physically approximate (misses afterglow chemistry)

  VERDICT: Fastest working approach but physically incomplete.

3.3 Unified RHS (no operator splitting) — PHYSICALLY CORRECT

  Single constrained BDF for entire pulse period
  All chemistry active including afterglow
  
  Performance: 11,000 RHS/pulse, 266 hours
  
  VERDICT: Physically correct but impractically slow.

3.4 solve_ivp(BDF) single call — UNBOUNDED

  Single solve_ivp for many pulses, no clamping
  
  Issues:
    - n_e goes negative (no constraint enforcement)
    - "Required step size < machine epsilon" at pulse edges
    - Works with rise_time=10µs but gives cycle-averaged result (3 RHS/pulse)
    
  VERDICT: Either fails or doesn't resolve pulses.

3.5 CVODE (sksundae) with ReInit

  Single CVODE instance, init_step at each pulse edge
  Non-negativity constraints available
  
  Issues:
    - init_step Python overhead: ~10ms per call × 680k calls = 2 hours overhead alone
    - Without analytical Jac: even slower (FD)
    - With analytical Jac: same Newton convergence failure as scipy BDF
    
  VERDICT: Python wrapper overhead kills performance.

3.6 Macro-timestep solver

  Resolve N_detail cycles, skip N_skip pulses with extrapolated source terms
  
  Issues:
    - Forward Euler: overshoots (conv=96% instead of 1.44%)
    - Exponential integrator: oscillates (conv=50%)
    - CSTR-aware analytical: still oscillates
    - Needs implicit macro-step (Newton on cycle-averaged equations)
    
  VERDICT: Infrastructure exists but needs implicit scheme.


4. JACOBIAN CONDITIONING PROBLEM
=================================

4.1 The numbers

  Jacobian J has shape (67, 67)
  Nonzero entries: 570/4489 (13%) — dense enough that sparse FD gives only 1.1× speedup
  Condition number: ~10^75
  cond(I - h*J): ~10^39 at h=1e-9

4.2 Why it's so poorly conditioned

  The Jacobian couples:
    - Electron density (c_e ~ 1e-16 mol/m³) with neutral species (c_N2 ~ 28 mol/m³)
      → 17 orders of magnitude difference in concentrations
    - Energy equation (ne_eps ~ 1e10 eV/m³) with species equations
    - Fast processes (attachment τ~5ns) with slow processes (CSTR flow τ~34s)
      → 10 orders of magnitude in timescales

  The diagonal elements span:
    - ∂(dc_e/dt)/∂c_e ≈ -1e8 (fast attachment loss)
    - ∂(dc_N2/dt)/∂c_N2 ≈ -0.03 (slow flow loss)
    - ∂(d(ne_eps)/dt)/∂c_e ≈ 1e34 (energy equation coupling)

4.3 Why analytical Jacobian fails

  Newton iteration solves: (I - h*J) × δy = -F(y)
  With cond(I - h*J) ≈ 10^39, the solution δy has ~39 digits of error
  Double precision (16 digits) → solution is pure noise
  → Newton never converges → BDF halves step size indefinitely

  FD Jacobian "works" because the FD errors happen to regularize the matrix:
    - FD perturbation size ~ sqrt(ε_mach) × |y_i| ≈ 1e-8 × |y_i|
    - This effectively smooths out the extreme off-diagonal entries
    - The resulting J_FD is less accurate but better conditioned

4.4 Potential solutions

  a) Jacobian scaling (row/column equilibration):
     D_r × J × D_c → D_r and D_c are diagonal scaling matrices
     D_r[i] = 1/max(|J[i,:]|), D_c[j] = 1/max(|J[:,j]|)
     Reduces condition number significantly
     Used in SUNDIALS CVODE internally (SUNLinSolScaledSetup)
     
  b) Variable scaling:
     Transform y_i → y_i / y_scale_i where y_scale_i is typical magnitude
     e.g., scale c_e by 1e-16, c_N2 by 28, ne_eps by 1e10
     Makes all scaled variables O(1) → Jacobian entries comparable
     
  c) Preconditioned Newton:
     Instead of solving (I - hJ)δy = -F directly,
     use P⁻¹(I - hJ)δy = -P⁻¹F where P is an approximate Jacobian
     e.g., P = diagonal of (I - hJ)
     
  d) Semi-implicit methods:
     Implicit only for stiff subsystem (electrons), explicit for slow (neutrals)
     IMEX (Implicit-Explicit) Runge-Kutta methods
     ARK methods in SUNDIALS


5. ENERGY EQUATION BEHAVIOR IN PULSED MODE
===========================================

5.1 Current model: d(ne·ε̄)/dt = P_dep - losses

  This is the same approach as GlobalKin (Stafford & Kushner 2004).
  P_dep is externally prescribed from Lissajous/pulse waveform.
  
  During ON: P_dep > 0, energy balance → Te ≈ 2.5 eV, n_e determined by balance
  During ramp-down: P_dep decreases, n_e decreases proportionally (Te stays ~constant)
  At P_dep=0: losses drain ne_eps → Te collapses to thermal in ~1-50 ns
  
  This behavior is physically correct for atmospheric pressure
  (Bieniek 2021, Lepikhin 2017).

5.2 Afterglow EI rate handling

  BEFORE fix: eps < eps_min_lut → k_ei = None → ALL EI rates = 0
    → 3-body attachment (σ/N, peaks at 0.08eV thermal) was disabled
    → Non-physical: detachment dominated, n_e recovered to 8.8e12

  AFTER fix: Maxwellian fallback for eps < eps_min_lut
    → 3-body attachment active at thermal Te
    → Correct physics: n_e decays to ~1e8 (floor) via attachment
    → Continuous mode unaffected (eps always in LUT range)

5.3 E/N vs P_dep prescription

  ZDPlasKin: E/N prescription → BOLSIG+ computes EEDF → Te is output
  GlobalKin: P_dep prescription → energy balance → Te is output
  Our model: P_dep prescription (same as GlobalKin)
  
  Both approaches are valid. P_dep allows direct use of Lissajous measurements.
  E/N approach requires inferring E/N from P_dep iteratively (possible but adds complexity).
  
  The key requirement: ALL loss terms must remain active at ALL Te values.
  This is now satisfied with the Maxwellian fallback.
